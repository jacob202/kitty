# Kitty Onboarding - Master Record

This document contains Jacob's comprehensive self-assessment and strategy for building a stable, coherent life.

## 1. Core Identity & Values

**Q: Who are you and what are you trying to build in your life right now?**
A: I am a 38-year-old gay man in recovery rebuilding my life after long-term addiction, isolation, disrupted functioning, and repeated unfinished starts. I am highly analytical, emotionally intense, repair-minded, and driven by a need for truth, connection, dignity, and practical competence.

My core challenge is not lack of insight. It is converting insight into stable repeated action. I can think deeply, notice patterns, and imagine complex systems, but I need to close the gap between internal potential and external proof.

I am trying to build a life based on recovery, stability, visible competence, and practical autonomy. That means becoming someone who continues instead of constantly restarting. It means building routines, repairing my health, earning back trust in myself, and proving through action that my life can become coherent again.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is continuity.

**Q: What does “getting your life back on track” actually mean to you?**
A: It means creating a stable foundation where recovery, health, hygiene, sleep, work capacity, relationships, and daily routines are no longer treated as optional. It means turning my ability to analyze into real-world results: finished tasks, kept appointments, cleaner spaces, repaired objects, consistent habits, and gradual financial independence.

It means I stop living in crisis, fantasy, avoidance, or shame. I start building a life that has structure, momentum, and proof.

**Q: What are your non-negotiables?**
A: My non-negotiables are recovery, dignity, truth, directness, and authenticity. I do not want vague motivational language, fake positivity, theatrical harshness, or clinical-sounding nonsense. I need reality-testing, clean reasoning, and practical next steps.

I also need to protect my recovery. Anything that destabilizes sobriety, sleep, basic health, or emotional regulation has to be treated seriously.

**Q: What is the biggest gap between who you are and who you want to be?**
A: The biggest gap is between my internal intensity and my external consistency. I can care deeply, analyze deeply, and imagine a better life, but I often struggle to translate that into repeated action. Avoidance, shame, overwhelm, and unfinished projects have made my external life smaller than my actual capacity.

Closing that gap means building proof: one kept routine, one finished task, one repaired thing, one honest conversation, one stable day at a time.

## 3. Health & Recovery

**Q: What are your current health conditions or ongoing issues?**
A: I am in recovery and managing the physical, emotional, and practical aftermath of addiction, isolation, disrupted routines, and long-term instability. My health is not a side issue. It is the foundation everything else depends on.

When my health basics collapse, everything else becomes harder: focus, mood, motivation, hygiene, relationships, appointments, and decision-making.

**Q: What medications or supplements are you taking?**
A: I take prescribed medication including:
- Testosterone
- Trazodone
- An antidepressant starting with "e" (e.g., Escitalopram/Lexapro or Effexor)
- One medication at a 56mg dose.

I have also used large supplement stacks, including many supplements aimed at cognitive support, neuroprotection, mitochondrial support, and focus.

This is a risk area because I can over-optimize and overcomplicate. The safe approach is to track what I am taking, simplify when needed, identify possible interaction questions, and bring medication or supplement concerns to a pharmacist or doctor. I should not make complex dose or combination changes impulsively.

**Q: How would you describe your sleep?**
A: Sleep stabilization is one of my highest priorities. When my sleep is stable, I have a much better chance of staying emotionally regulated, following routines, managing cravings, and continuing from one day to the next.

When sleep collapses, I become more vulnerable to spiraling, avoidance, emotional fixation, impulsive decisions, and giving up on structure.

**Q: What are your main health goals right now?**
A: My main health goals are physical continuity, recovery protection, and baseline stability. I need to treat food, water, hygiene, medication consistency, sleep, movement, and appointments as infrastructure, not optional self-improvement extras.

The goal is not to become perfect overnight. The goal is to stop letting the basics collapse.

## 4. Recovery Protection

**Q: What warning signs should be taken seriously?**
A: Serious warning signs include:
- not sleeping
- not eating
- skipping prescribed medication
- isolating
- craving substances
- obsessively looping on one person, project, or problem
- letting hygiene collapse
- talking like nothing matters
- using shame as motivation
- avoiding appointments or responsibilities
- repeatedly expanding a simple task into a life-sized crisis

**Q: What should happen when recovery feels unstable?**
A: Long-range planning should stop temporarily. The focus should shift to immediate stabilization:
- eat something real
- drink water
- take prescribed medication as directed, or address why I am not
- shower or do basic hygiene
- clean one small area
- contact a real support person if needed
- reduce stimulation
- sleep or make a sleep plan
- complete one small concrete task only if appropriate

Recovery comes before productivity. If I sacrifice recovery to chase intensity, I am not making progress. I am setting up another collapse.

## 6. Productivity & Work Style

**Q: How would you describe your work style?**
A: I can hyperfocus deeply on complex problems, especially when they involve systems, repair, technical details, or high personal meaning. But I am vulnerable to overwhelm when the scope expands too much.

When overwhelmed, I may avoid the next concrete step by making the problem bigger, more abstract, or more emotionally loaded. I can turn a practical task into a full identity crisis. This causes scattered focus, abandoned projects, and shame.

I need continuity mechanisms: clear next steps, visible task lists, short work blocks, and realistic stopping points.

**Q: What does a good day look like?**
A: A good day is built from proof, not intensity.
A good day might include:
- one kept appointment
- one 25-minute focused block
- one completed repair step
- one cleaned area
- one honest message
- one meal
- one walk or movement session
- one written next step for tomorrow

A good day does not require solving my whole life.

**Q: What does a bad day look like?**
A: A bad day is when the gap between my potential and my reality feels too wide and I retreat into avoidance, isolation, fantasy, overthinking, or shame. I may stop doing basics because I feel like if I cannot do everything, nothing matters.

That pattern has to be interrupted quickly.

**Q: What are your biggest time wasters or drains?**
A: My biggest drains are:
- expanding problems instead of doing the next step
- over-planning
- chasing the perfect system
- restarting instead of continuing
- shame loops over past failure
- emotional fixation
- avoidance disguised as analysis
- letting one broken routine destroy the whole day
- working from intensity instead of structure

## 7. Decision Rules

**Q: How should decisions be made when you are stuck?**
A: Use simple rules unless the stakes are genuinely high.
Default rules:
- If a task takes under 10 minutes, do it before analyzing it.
- If there are more than three options, reduce them to the top two.
- If the risk is low, choose the option that creates momentum fastest.
- If there is no irreversible downside, act.
- If perfection appears, make version 0.1.
- If the task feels huge, define the next physical action.
- If I am overwhelmed, reduce scope immediately.
- If I miss a day, resume the next day instead of redesigning my life.

**Q: What question cuts through avoidance fastest?**
A: “What is the next visible action I can complete in 10–25 minutes?”
